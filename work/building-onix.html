Building Onix to Automate ESP Drop Creation

Case study · Platform / direct-ESP integration

By the time I started working on what would become Onix, the upstream half of the campaign pipeline was already automated — an earlier Python project of mine (Viper) was writing the upcoming schedule into a shared Google Sheet that the team used as its source of truth. The bottleneck had moved downstream. Getting the rows on that shared schedule turned into actual sends was still a manual job, and it lived with the operations team — well over fifty people who would, every day, take each row and configure the corresponding campaign in our ESPs. They worked through a mix of the ESP user interfaces directly and an internal legacy platform that another team at the company had built to give them a unified view across ESP accounts.

It was not a workflow that scaled. The legacy platform was buggy and the fix cycle on those bugs was long. The user interface required several lookups just to find the offer you were trying to schedule against. The ops team did its best, but with that many people setting up that many drops every day, the failure mode was structural — wrong creative going out, wrong segment targeted, send going out at the wrong time, occasional drops that simply did not go at all. None of those errors were on any one person; they were a consequence of asking humans to do high-volume, high-precision data entry across multiple inconsistent systems.

Around the same time, the legacy platform was being deprecated and the operations team's work was being scaled down. The need was clear: a system that could take a schedule from the shared sheet and create the corresponding drops directly in the ESPs, without depending on either the legacy platform or human ops setup.

What I built

Onix is a Python service that reads from the shared schedule, decides per drop which ESP that account uses, and creates the campaign in that ESP via its API. Today it covers Iterable and Pinpointe; the architecture is open enough that adding a third ESP would not require rewriting the core.

The migration off the old setup happened account by account. For each account, I validated Onix's flow against what the legacy setup had been producing, fixed anything that did not line up, and moved that account fully onto Onix once it was clean. Drop creation is a different kind of risky than drop content rendering — a wrong segment or a wrong send time is a customer-visible mistake that you cannot quietly fix after the fact — so the migration could not happen in a single switchover for any individual account.

The hardest problem: speaking two ESP languages without losing the meaning of either

The most interesting engineering problem in Onix was the domain model. The two ESPs we integrate with have fundamentally different concepts of what a "drop" is.

In Iterable, a campaign and its content template are two separate objects. You build the content template once, you create a campaign that points at it, and the campaign carries the segmentation and send-time information separately from the content. In Pinpointe, a "newsletter" is both the campaign and the content template at the same time — they are not separable concepts in Pinpointe's API.

The shared schedule, meanwhile, has its own language. A row on it describes a drop — which on the surface is simpler than either ESP's model, but in practice has to be precise enough to map cleanly onto whichever ESP the account uses, and general enough to leave room for a third ESP one day.

So the question was: how do you design the abstractions inside Onix so that the generic layer can reason about drops the way the shared schedule does, while the ESP-specific layer talks in each ESP's native terms?

What I landed on is a domain layer that defines what a drop is in our own language — segment, send time, offer, creative, suppression list, brand context — and a set of ESP adapters that know how to translate that into the specific shape each ESP expects. The Iterable adapter knows that a drop becomes a content template plus a campaign and that the campaign carries the schedule. The Pinpointe adapter knows that a drop becomes a single newsletter object and that the abstraction we use elsewhere does not map cleanly to that — and the adapter handles the translation rather than leaking the inconsistency upward.

The discipline I held to was: any code outside the adapters should be able to read like our internal language. Whether a given account is on Iterable or Pinpointe should only matter at the boundary, never in the core. That has held up well — the place I most see it pay off is when something needs to change in how we handle drops generally, and the change happens in one place rather than two-with-an-asterisk.

Suppression

The other thing Onix does at drop creation time is enforce suppression. There are two layers.

The first is per-offer suppression. Most of the offers we run come with MD5-hash lists from the advertiser of users they do not want messaged. Onix downloads those lists, cross-references them against the segment for each drop, and removes any matches before the drop is created. This used to be a manual step, when it was done at all.

The second is what I call global suppression — a list of users we never want to send to under any circumstances, for our own reasons. I built an internal API endpoint that other people at the company can post MD5 hashes to when a user needs to be added to that list. Two things keep this list enforced: every segment Onix builds is filtered against it at drop creation, and a pair of Cloud Run jobs (one for each ESP) runs on a schedule to find and unsubscribe anyone already sitting in an ESP segment who happens to match the global list. The first is preemptive; the second is a continuous safety net. Many users on the global list have never appeared in any of our segments at all — the system catches them either way.

Building Onix with AI tools

Onix was the first significant system I built where AI coding assistants were a meaningful part of how I worked. Viper, by contrast, was mostly hand-written, with AI assistance coming in only years later for additions and refactors. Onix was AI-assisted from earlier in its lifecycle.

In practice, my workflow with the tools has two modes. For new architectural questions — like how to model the Iterable / Pinpointe difference cleanly — I would describe the concept in plain language, ask for a few possible shapes of the abstraction, and then pick or compose from what came back. The AI was good at proposing options I had not thought of; I was better at knowing which option would survive contact with the actual ESPs over time. For more concrete pieces of code, I would often write the function myself, or to maybe ninety percent completeness, and then hand it to the AI to clean up, add documentation comments, and place into the appropriate section of the codebase. That second mode probably accounts for more of my actual AI use than the first.

Either way, the division of labor is the same: generative breadth from the tool, judgment from me.

This only works, though, when the codebase gives the AI something solid to ground its suggestions in. Onix was scaffolded with that in mind from the start: clear module boundaries, deliberate naming for the ESP adapters, tests written alongside the code rather than retrofitted. Some of that is just good engineering. A piece of it is specifically about making the codebase legible to the assistant I planned to keep working with.

What I'd think harder about next time

The biggest thing I'd do differently is be more firm and more descriptive when telling the AI what to do — particularly around the abstractions I wanted in place.

A few corners of Onix are organized in ways that work fine functionally but read less intuitively than they should to a human. There are workflows, for example, that require an ESP class to be created or a Drop class to be instantiated, and conceptually those are not the most accurate ways to think about what is happening. Everything runs correctly. The code is just slightly harder to read than it needs to be in those places, because at the time I let the AI propose the structure and I did not push back hard enough on what felt off.

The thing I learned is that with AI tools, the failure mode usually isn't "the AI produced bad code." It's "the AI produced code that works but doesn't match the mental model I actually wanted." Catching that means going into the prompt with a clear picture of what the abstractions should be, and pushing back when what comes back doesn't fit. I came out of Onix more disciplined about that than I went in.
