Building Viper to Automate Email Scheduling

Case study · Platform / automation

I was hired into my current company as a digital marketing analyst. The work was a mix of Tableau dashboards and historical analysis — looking at what had worked, what hadn't, and using that to inform decisions about upcoming sends. The decisions themselves landed in a shared Google Sheet that the team used as the scheduling source of truth. Each of us was assigned somewhere between 10 and 20 accounts, and our job for those was to fill in the upcoming schedule: which audience segment to send to (something like last 30-day openers), what time of day, which offer to run, and which specific email creative under that offer.

The reason this work landed on human analysts in the first place is that the decision is more complicated than it sounds. A good schedule has to respect the gap between two sends of the same offer to an account, the gap between two sends of the same creative type, day-of-week restrictions, seasonality, the ongoing experimentation we wanted to run with new offers, and the occasional re-introduction of an older offer that had performed well at some point and might again. There were dozens of interacting constraints and recent-performance signals; the people doing the job were genuinely making judgment calls every time. The work was slow because the work was hard.

But it also did not scale. We were doing this for every assigned account, every week, for a network that at peak reached around 35 million sends a day. Manual data entry leaks errors regardless of how careful you are, and we were slowly losing time that could have been spent working on other projects within the company.

How it got built

I floated the idea of automating the process. The response was reasonable skepticism. Marketing sends are sensitive — these are emails going to real people, at scale, and getting a decision wrong can mean off-brand creative, fatigued audiences, or worse. Nobody wanted that experiment to happen on live production sends, and they had a point.

So I built it in my free time, alongside my analyst work, on accounts I owned. I called the project Viper — it was a set of Python scripts working alongside our existing snake-themed scheduling pipeline.

From first version to current architecture

The first useful version of Viper covered one or two accounts and tried to reproduce the kind of decisions I would have made for them by hand. I let it pick segment, time, offer, and creative for the upcoming week, then compared what it picked against what I would have picked. When the answers matched (with explainable disagreements), I let it actually run those decisions on those accounts. When they didn't, I dug into why and either fixed the logic or accepted that the difference was actually an improvement from what I might have selected.

From there it iterated. Two accounts became ten. Ten became all of my assigned accounts. Eventually it was producing schedules that, when compared against what other analysts were producing for similar accounts, were as good or better — which was the moment it stopped being a personal productivity tool and started being a candidate for the team's actual workflow.

Today, Viper writes the full schedule for the entire portfolio (around 285 accounts) into the same shared sheet the team has used from the beginning. I deliberately kept that sheet in the middle of the pipeline even after Viper was doing the heavy lifting on decisions. It serves three purposes: it's an authoritative log of what's planned, it gives people outside the Viper workflow a place to make ad-hoc manual edits when they need to, and it gives me a fast experimentation surface for whatever I'm building downstream. The sheet is essentially the system's UI — Viper writes into it, humans can edit it, downstream systems read from it.

The architecture is also deliberately pluggable. Different accounts can be configured to use different content-selection functions, offer-ranking functions, and scheduling-rule sets. I built it this way partly because I imagined other engineers or analysts might eventually write their own logic to plug in alongside mine, and partly because I wanted to be able to try new selection logic on individual accounts without disturbing the central engine. The first reason has stayed hypothetical so far — I am still the one writing the logic — but the second is exactly how I A/B test any new functions that I want to try today.

The implementation itself evolved in steps that each made sense at the time. The very first version was a Jupyter notebook I ran by hand on my laptop. The notebook became a regular Python project, then got tests, a CI pipeline, a Docker image, a managed-cloud deployment triggered on a schedule, and a GitHub Action that redeploys the image automatically whenever I push to main. The data layer connects to multiple SQL databases. Quality holds together through automated Slack alerts I wired up early — bringing an engineering-style monitoring pattern into the analyst workflow for the first time — a separate automated scanner that checks the schedule sheet for errors and offers that have run out of budget and need to be swapped out, and a steadily growing pytest suite. None of those evolutions happened because they sounded interesting in isolation. Each one happened because the version before it had become the thing slowing me down or, worse, the thing that was occasionally failing in ways that touched real sends. The shape the system has today is the shape that operational pressure produced.

A principle I think generalizes

The single most important thing about how Viper got built is that I was already the user. I had spent months filling in those rows by hand before I started automating any of it. I knew what decisions felt automatic, what decisions were genuinely judgment calls, what the recurring mistakes were, and which accounts had quirks that needed special handling. None of that lived in a requirements document. It lived in my head because I was the one doing the work.

I think this generalizes. Software built by people who use the software they build tends to be more proactive — anticipating cases the developer has personally encountered — than software built by people who don't. The second category tends to be reactive by structure: the user finds the problem, files a ticket, the developer fixes that one case, repeat. The first category catches whole classes of problems before they happen because the developer has felt them.

I would not call this an original observation, but it does seem to be becoming rarer as engineering roles get more specialized and the people writing the code drift further from the people using it. Better tooling and better PM-to-engineer translation help, but they don't fully replace having actually done the work yourself. The Viper experience is the strongest case I can make for closing that distance when you can.

What I'd think harder about next time

The biggest thing I'd do differently is invest in a real test suite much earlier. Viper had automated Slack alerts and an automated scheduling scanner in place fairly early — the observability side of quality was actually never the gap. What was the gap, for too long, was tests.

I did not know about pytest when I started, and by the time I came around to taking testing seriously, there was already a substantial amount of code that had grown without tests acting as a contract on its behavior. Two costs came out of that. The obvious one was bugs that real tests would have caught earlier. The less obvious one — and the one I think about more now — is that written tests function as documentation of intent, and that documentation is something the AI tooling I lean on today reads and uses. AI assistants are very good at writing additional tests once the right shape exists; they are not good at inferring the right shape from a codebase that has none. If I had written a substantial test suite myself from scratch in the early days, every AI-assisted change I have made since would have had a sturdier foundation to build on, and I would have spent less time correcting context the tools were missing.

The lesson I'd carry into any future system is that testing isn't a finishing touch. It's the floor under everything else — and the artifact that makes collaborating on the codebase later, whether with humans or with AI, actually work.
