Replacing a Manual Templating Workflow with an In-House Rendering Engine

Case study · Rendering / content quality

After Onix took over drop creation, the last piece of the pipeline that still needed hands was the templates themselves. The HTML for each send was hand-built by the operations team in a legacy editor that lived inside the same internal platform Onix had largely moved off of. The ops team assembled the template, manually attached CTA links to the right offers, and were supposed to verify everything before sending — typos, grammar, every link still working.

In practice, that verification often didn't happen, and even when it did, the legacy system had bugs the ops team couldn't work around. One example sticks with me: emails would routinely go out with floating commas like "Hi ," instead of "Hi John,". The merge-tag handling didn't deal gracefully with missing first names. It had been an outstanding request for years before I owned the rendering layer. Once I did, it took a day. That one fix is small, but it shows the broader pattern — the legacy templating workflow had accumulated quality issues the people running it had no way to address.

When the legacy platform was deprecated and the operations team scaled down, the templating work had to come in-house. That is when I built the rendering engine into Onix.

What I built

The renderer reads body content from a separate Google Sheet that serves as our content warehouse, applies a brand-specific Jinja shell, attaches CTA links, adds unsubscribe links, and handles legal-disclaimer logic. Onix calls it at drop creation time, so the final HTML is produced per send, on the fly.

The brand shell is deliberately separate from the body content and configurable per brand, which means I can update the look of every send for a given brand by changing one Jinja template rather than editing every row of content. The shell handles dark-mode treatments, header and footer styling, contextual unsubscribe affordances, and the small mechanical details (copyright-year refresh, normalized trailing punctuation, small-font legal text inferred from the rich-text metadata of the sheet) that would otherwise be long-tail polish problems an ops team would have to remember to do by hand.

The migration off the legacy templating was less involved than it would have been if there had been any visual variety to preserve. The legacy system produced essentially one standard look across every brand and every send — frankly, it is a wonder anyone ever clicked anything. I rolled brands onto the new engine in waves, watched closely for anything unexpected, and fixed what needed fixing.

CTA detection

One of the harder problems in the renderer was figuring out which text in the body content should become the CTA link. In the legacy workflow, a human read the content and manually decided what to hyperlink. Doing it that way at the scale we render now was not an option.

The obvious approach would have been to let an AI model scan each body and pick the CTA. I deliberately did not want to do that. Running a model over every piece of content at render time is expensive, opaque, and slow to debug when it picks the wrong thing. Instead, I used AI to build the detection, not to run it: I started with a short list of heuristics — specific verb patterns, common CTA phrasing, position within the body, surrounding markup — and iterated them with AI assistance, sampling failures and adding rules until the detection was reliable on every send.

That choice — heuristics at runtime, AI at design time — has held up well. The detection is fast, deterministic, and when it picks wrong I can read the rules and see why. That feels like the right way to use AI tooling for anything that runs in production — let it help you find a rule set, then run the rule set yourself.

The validation gate

The renderer refuses to render any row of content that is flagged for quality issues. Quality flagging happens in the content warehouse itself — a Google Apps Script I wrote runs nightly across every row and produces a "Verify Flags" column. The checks cover length, encoding (Unicode replacement characters from copy-paste corruption), merge-tag validity, footnote consistency, missing privacy-policy links, literal years that should be dynamic, duplicate lines within a body, spelling against a maintained whitelist, and grammar via a self-hosted LanguageTool service.

I built the validator inside the sheet, where the content team actually writes the copy, on purpose. Putting validation in front of the human who can fix the problem in real time is much cheaper than catching it later at render time, when context has gone cold and there is operational pressure to send. The renderer's refusal to render flagged content is the hard guarantee underneath. The validator inside the sheet is the way most flags get resolved before the renderer ever sees them.

The merge-tag work is part of this layer. Beyond the "Hi ," fix, the validator also catches unclosed merge-tag blocks, bare field references that would render with a floating comma if the field were empty, and unrecognized merge syntax. The class of bugs that produced "Hi ," in the legacy system is structurally impossible now.

Challenges

The largest technical challenge was working with the content warehouse sheet at the scale we operate. The warehouse holds roughly 38,000 rows of body content and grows. Pulling it down, scanning every row, and writing results back every night is a job that has to be designed around the Google Sheets API's quotas and pagination behavior. Naive implementations time out or fail partway through. The current setup chunks reads and writes, caches what it can, and treats the sheet itself as the source of truth — which keeps the operational picture simple but requires being deliberate about how much work the script does per run.

Legal disclaimers were the more sensitive challenge, even though they were less hard technically. Different offers require different legal footers, and the specification of which footer applies was inconsistent in our data — sometimes in the body content, sometimes attached at the offer level in metadata, sometimes both, occasionally with different versions. Missing or wrong legal text can have real regulatory consequences. The renderer treats the offer-level specification as the source of truth, falls back to body-level when needed, and flags any inconsistency for me to review directly. It is not the most elegant solution, but it matches the actual state of the upstream data and keeps the legal exposure under control.

What the new system unlocks

The thing I find most exciting about the new setup is that the brand-shell layer makes new visual styles cheap. With the legacy templating workflow, every send looked the same, regardless of advertiser. With the new engine, I can take an advertiser's brand and have a beautiful, advertiser-specific template ready within about ten minutes. That has changed what kinds of campaigns we can run.

What I'd think harder about next time

The biggest thing I'd do differently is unify the sheet's content rules and the renderer's logic from the start, rather than letting them grow as two layers that occasionally disagree.

In the current setup, the content warehouse sheet has a dedicated CTA column where the explicit CTA text can be written. At first, that column was only a fallback — used when the renderer's heuristics failed to find a CTA, or when someone needed to override what the heuristics picked. Over time it became clear that having two ways to know what the CTA was meant occasional surprises: the renderer would pick something the column did not agree with, or the heuristics would miss entirely and a drop would error out at render time.

If I started over, the column would be the source of truth from day one and the renderer would just read from it. The heuristics still have a place — they help fill in the column quickly when bulk-onboarding new content — but inferring at runtime is the kind of cleverness that catches up with you. The general principle is that any system with two ways of arriving at the same answer will eventually find a way to disagree with itself, and the cheaper move is to pick one source of truth as early as possible.
